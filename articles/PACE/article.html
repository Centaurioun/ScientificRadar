The field of gene editing has witnessed a remarkable leap forward with recent advancements in prime editing technology. The study by Jordan L. Doman and colleagues, published in Cell, details the innovative use of phage-assisted continuous evolution (PACE) and meticulous protein engineering to create compact, efficient prime editors that hold immense promise for genetic research and therapy. In this article, we will delve deeper into the intricacies of these advancements, their implications for therapeutic gene editing, and the challenges they address in gene delivery.

Pushing the Boundaries of gene editing

prime editing, a technique that enables precise DNA editing without double-stranded breaks associated with CRISPR-Cas9, has been further refined to increase its efficiency and applicability. The research led by Doman et al. showcases the evolution of compact reverse transcriptases (RTs) that significantly enhance the editing capabilities of prime editors. These advancements were achieved through a combination of PACE and rational protein engineering, resulting in the creation of editors that are not only more efficient but also smaller in size, making them ideal for therapeutic applications.

prime editing

A revolutionary gene editing technique that combines the precision of CRISPR-Cas9 with the efficiency of a reverse transcriptase to directly write new genetic information into a specific DNA site. Unlike traditional CRISPR-Cas9, prime editing does not rely on double-stranded DNA breaks, reducing the risk of unwanted mutations and increasing its potential for therapeutic use.

phage-assisted continuous evolution (PACE) and protein engineering

phage-assisted continuous evolution is a powerful technique that allows for the rapid evolution of proteins with desired properties. By combining PACE with rational protein engineering, researchers can transcend the limitations of wild-type enzymes and generate highly efficient, compact prime editors. In the case of Doman et al.'s study, PACE was employed to improve the editing efficiencies of compact RTs by up to 22-fold, resulting in the development of prime editors that are significantly more efficient and compact than their predecessors.

phage-assisted continuous evolution (PACE)

A method of evolving proteins or nucleic acids at an accelerated pace by linking their function to the propagation of bacteriophages. This technique allows for the rapid discovery of genetic variants with desirable properties, such as increased efficiency or specificity, by simulating a high-pressure evolutionary environment.

The Evolution of Compact Reverse Transcriptases (RTs)

Reverse transcriptases play a crucial role in the prime editing process, as they are responsible for converting the RNA template into DNA. In their quest to enhance the efficiency of prime editors, Doman et al. surveyed 59 enzymes from 14 different classes of RTs. While they found that diverse RTs can support prime editing, the wild-type forms of these enzymes do not mediate efficient editing in mammalian cells. Even the most active dual-AAV-compatible compact RTs showed lower editing efficiency compared to the full-length RT in PE2 when installing long, complex edits.

To address these challenges, the team leveraged the power of protein engineering and PACE. They discovered that different reverse transcriptases specialize in different types of edits and used this insight to create RTs that outperform both PEmax and PEmax ΔRNaseH, the truncated editor commonly used in dual-AAV delivery systems for in vivo applications. This breakthrough led to the development of the PE6 series of prime editors, which offer significant improvements in editing efficiency and compactness.

Reverse Transcriptases (RTs)

Enzymes that convert RNA into DNA, playing a pivotal role in the life cycle of retroviruses. In the context of prime editing, RTs are fused to a CRISPR-Cas9 system to template the desired DNA changes directly into the target site, allowing for precise gene correction without the need for donor DNA templates.

adeno-associated virus (AAV) Delivery

A widely used gene therapy method due to its low pathogenicity and ability to deliver genetic material to non-dividing cells. AAV vectors are engineered viruses that can carry therapeutic genes or gene-editing tools like CRISPR-Cas9 systems into human cells, making them effective vehicles for treating genetic disorders.

A Versatile Toolkit for Genetic Modifications

The study introduces several key innovations in the realm of prime editing. Through the strategic evolution of RTs, the researchers developed variants that specialize in different types of edits, offering a toolkit of editors optimized for various genetic modifications. Among these, the PE6 series stands out, offering editors like PE6a and PE6b, which significantly outperform their predecessors in editing efficiency and compactness.

The PE6 Series: A Leap Forward in prime editing

The PE6 series of prime editors represents a major advancement in genome editing technology. These highly efficient, compact prime editors have been shown to enhance therapeutically relevant editing in patient-derived fibroblasts and primary human T cells. Moreover, PE6 variants enable the installation of longer insertions in vivo following dual-AAV delivery. In a remarkable feat, they achieved 40% loxP sequence insertion in the cortex of mouse brains - a 24-fold improvement over previous cutting-edge prime editors.

The PE6 series has shown remarkable success in editing complex genomic sites and holds great potential for therapeutic gene editing, with applications demonstrated in correcting mutations associated with diseases like Tay-Sachs. The development of these compact, hyper-efficient PE6 prime editors through phage-assisted evolution and protein engineering represents a key milestone in the field of gene editing, expanding the horizons of what is possible with this technology.

Implications for Therapeutic gene editing and Overcoming Challenges in Gene Delivery

The implications of these advancements are profound, especially in the context of therapeutic gene editing. The compact size of the newly developed prime editors makes them suitable for delivery via AAVs, overcoming one of the major hurdles in gene therapy. Moreover, their enhanced efficiency and specificity in editing complex genetic sites open new avenues for treating a wide range of genetic disorders with unprecedented precision.

One of the most significant challenges in gene therapy has been the delivery of gene editing tools into the target cells efficiently and safely. The development of compact prime editors, as reported by Doman et al., addresses this challenge head-on. By reducing the size of these editors, they become compatible with AAV delivery methods, which are known for their safety and efficiency in delivering genetic material to cells. This breakthrough not only expands the potential applications of gene editing therapies but also enhances their feasibility and accessibility.

The recent advancements in prime editing technology, particularly the development of compact and efficient prime editors through innovative protein engineering and phage-assisted evolution, hold immense promise for the future of genetic research and therapy. These groundbreaking editors offer a versatile toolkit for genetic modifications, enabling more precise and effective gene editing with minimal off-target effects. By overcoming challenges in gene delivery and paving the way for new therapeutic applications, these advancements bring us one step closer to unlocking the full potential of gene editing for the betterment of human health.
